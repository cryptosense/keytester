Results

Small factors
RSA public keys are supposed to be the product of two large prime numbers. If a public key has a small divisor, it means that it was generated with a flawed algorithm.
Common factors
Factoring a single key is difficult, but in practice, with a large number of keys at hand, it is sometimes possible to find a common factor between keys. If such a factor exists, both keys can be factored.

FAQ

1. What does batch-GCD do?

The security of RSA relies on the fact that factoring the public modulus of a key is computationally hard: for example, if the modulus is of the form p.q, where p and q are large primes, there is no known efficient algorithm for obtaining the factorisation. But suppose that we have a large number of keys available: we can try to compute the pairwise GCDs of these moduli. If two of them happen to share a common factor (that is, one is p.q and the other one is p.q’), their GCD will be p and it will be possible to factor these two keys, breaking security. Batch GCD applies this technqiue to a large number of RSA moduli in an efficient way.

2. How often are RSA keys vulnerable to this attack?

Given "perfect" random number generators, the probability of two large moduli sharing a prime is vanishingly small. However, in practice, faulty random number generators often lead to keys being vulnerable to this method of cryptanalysis. In 2012, two large-scale surveys of publically available RSA keys were carried out, resulting in tens of thousands of keys being factored. The failure rate of RSA keys was reported as 0.2% in Lenstra et al's work.

3. Can't I just do this test myself?

Certainly you can, and there is plenty of information and code around for doing it. However, for the test to be meaningful you need to do it against a large database of other RSA keys. That's what we've taken care of here at Cryptosense - we will apply batch GCD against your key and all the other keys in our (large) database.

4. I have a large number of keys generated by the same model of embedded device, wouldn't it make sense to batch-GCD them together rather than against your database?

You should definitely do that. Even better would be to do both. Get in touch if you need help to handle a large number of keys.

5. What about invalid keys, typos etc.?

Our web page includes a sanity check on the key: it tries to divide it by the first few primes. If there is a parse error or a factor is found, you will get a warning message and we won't run batch GCD on your key.
